RACHEL TYPRIN

I came to software engineering because I wanted to work on problems that help people live healthier and safer lives. The thread across my work is that hard problems are worth solving when the people on the other end of the solution actually feel the impact.

One of my current projects examines how regions of the brain connect and communicate. A practical application is helping neurosurgeons identify which areas to avoid during tumor removal to preserve cognitive and motor function. When I reviewed prior work, I wasn't convinced the existing models were finding the best solution. I developed three alternative models and found one that produced more accurate results across a broader range of patients. The stakes of getting that right are not abstract.

Neither is weather. I grew up in the San Francisco Bay Area, where conditions can shift dramatically within a few miles, but it wasn't until I moved to the East Coast for college that I realized most places don't work that way. Unreliable forecasting still catches me off guard when I'm back home. I ended up setting up a personal weather station just to get accurate predictions for my block. People shouldn't have to do that. So I'm building a microclimate forecasting model for the Bay Area that actually accounts for that variability.

My experience is rooted in human-centered design. I'm interested in how technology impacts the human experience. As part of this focus, I explored how digital spaces can influence emotional wellbeing, studying hopecore, a social media trend in which users share hopeful content to combat negativity.

I've also designed a discussion-based card game that helps people have structured conversations about life and death. I wanted to create an experience that doesn't avoid difficult and very human topics, but uses human-centered design to make them approachable.

Feel free to explore this portfolio and reach out if you are interested in solving problems together!
